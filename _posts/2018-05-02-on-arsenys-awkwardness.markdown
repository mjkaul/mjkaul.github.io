Who’s more awkward than a saint? 

Prophets are antagonistic by vocation—they’re *expected* to be awkward, which makes them less awkward. Saints imply by their *lives* rather than preach in sermons that I could—and therefore should—be doing better. You can write off a sermon, but a holy life puts you to shame. Awkward.

In his short, amusing book *Awkwardness*, Adam Kotsko defines the concept:

> Everyday awkwardness seems to originate with particular individuals. We are only able to identify someone as awkward because the person does something that is inappropriate for a given context. (6)

But awkwardness isn’t ever simply a character flaw. Awkwardness originates with individuals, but individual awkwardness is fundamentally social:

> Even when personal deficits make certain individuals seem extremely awkward by nature, awkwardness remains a social phenomenon, and therefore the analysis of awkwardness should focus not on awkward individuals but on the entire social situation in which awkwardness makes itself felt. (7)

The word itself means something like “clumsy movement” or “backwards turn”: *awke* means “backwards, perverse, clumsy”; *ward* is “movement” or “direction” from a Germanic base meaning “turn”. In *Laurus*, clear, forward movement is essentially completely absent, so “awkwardness” is a pretty usefully way to understand the form of the “novel” as a whole. 

Awkwardness is present in each of *Laurus*’s four books (though not in each portion of Arseny’s life, as we’ll see). Arseny, saintly from his childhood, is awkward. Here are a few of the instances of awkwardness that caught my eye.

---

The first is when he’s just six—he makes his herbalist-healer grandfather, Christopher, feel awkward when Arseny takes seriously a legend about the herb called “river crossing,” which is used to repel snakes but whose name implies that it can be used to part a river when you cross it. 

> It will part? asked Arseny, serious.

> With prayer it will part. Christofer began to feel awkward. Everything is about prayer, after all. (I.17)

This is a classic example of awkwardness—a six-year-old’s failure to understand the distinction between legend and reality. Arseny doesn’t quite grasp the natural order yet. But this fact is part of his gift, too: in the very next scene, Arseny befriends and adopts a wolf that appears and frightens Christopher. Sometimes what looks to adults like a failure turns out to be a gift. (That doesn’t make it any less awkward, however.)

---

The next occurrence of awkwardness is tragic. Arseny’s love and his common-law wife, Ustina, is about to give birth in their small, two-room cabin:

> The thought that she might leave forever took Arseny’s breath away. He had never considered that. The gravity of that thought turned out to be great. It dragged him down and he slipped from the bench onto the floor. It was as if he heard the faraway knock of a head on wood. He saw how awkwardly Ustina was rising from the bench and bending toward him. He saw everything. He was conscious but could not move. If he had known the gravity of that same thought before, his fear of speaking about Ustina in the quarter would have seemed laughable to him. (I.75)

Though Ustina is the awkward one here, that awkwardness is a sign that something is wrong with Arseny. Her awkward movements are a sign to him that he has turned the wrong way, first in waiting to marry her; second in refusing to get her a midwife. Both reflect Arseny’s pride: he naïvely assumes that he can handle the delivery and doesn’t want to take on the shame of letting the gossipy townspeople know about their sin. Arseny behaves awkwardly, and it’s reflected in the situation he has put Ustina in—an awkward situation in which she’s turned away from the natural path of marriage and midwife, with tragic consequences.

---

> They wrapped Arseny in a coverlet and he felt awkward before the residents of Ivachevo who gazed at him. Never before had he ridden in such a sledge. And he had not imagined the passage could be so comfortable. Or the motion so fast. (II.106)

This is the first instance of Arseny’s awkwardness vis-a-vis the community as a whole. Arseny understands what the people don’t: his ability to heal is not about him, about any particular skill or special gift that he has. He’s attentive, and works carefully and deliberately to heal. But he’s not always successful. He doesn’t (typically) work miracles, but rather simply brings good sense and, most importantly, hope. People begin to hear about him, to learn of his healing powers. His reputation spreads.

It’s this reputation as a healer that ultimately leads to the awkwardness here: this scene reveals how he begins to be treated differently, to be *revered*. In this scene, he’s whisked off to the city of Belozersk, to Prince Mikhail’s home, where he heals the prince’s family. The prince tries to retain Arseny as a sort of royal physician. Arseny leaves the palace as quickly as he’s able, and nearly as quickly gives up the fine fur coat the prince gifts him.

---

But while he’s in Belozersk, he uses the money he, for the first time, receives for his healing work in ways that reveal another aspect of awkwardness. Arseny begins to take German lessons from a Russian merchant:

> When Arseny learned [the German words’] meanings, it surprised him that familiar things could be expressed in such an unusual and—this was the main thing—awkward way. (II.119)

Why is their awkwardness “the main thing” about Arseny’s view of them? This question gets at a core issue in the book, the question of the “medieval synthesis.” 

The very existence of other languages opens up questions in Arseny’s mind about medieval “grand theory.” Vodolazkin is keen, throughout the book, to complicate our assumptions about the middle ages; this linguistic awkwardness is one way that he shows how cracks in the medieval synthesis would have been apparent to Arseny. Another is Arseny’s pre-scientific attempts, during this same period in his life, to distinguish between causation and correlation, cause and association. Through this large-scale awkwardness, Vodolazkin suggests that medievals, far from simply naïvely reflecting the unity and cohesion of “the medieval worldview,” possessed as much self-awareness and capacity for critique as we do in modernity.

---

So, awkwardness follows Arseny throughout his life, like a devoted dog (or, in Arseny’s case, wolf). Among all the phases of his strange and awkward life, the only period when there’s no mention of awkwardness whatsoever is when he is living as a holy fool in Pskov.

A person like Arseny—a healer, seen as a miracle worker; an aloof loner and wanderer—is awkward because he’s unusual, and it doesn’t really matter if he’s seen with suspicion or adoration: he’s still awkward, doing “things that are inappropriate in a given context” like healing nearly dead people and showing up unexpectedly in your town and building a makeshift home in your convent’s courtyard.

But such eccentricities—etymologically, “eccentric,” “away from center,” is similar to “awkward”—are core to the role of the holy fool. Arseny finds a peace and stability as a holy fool that he possesses at no other point in his life, and can live unselfconsciously and with seemingly no knowledge of time’s passage. Foma, one of two other holy fools in Pskov, at one point tells Arseny—now known as “Ustin”—that he has lived in Pskov as a holy fool for 21 years. This is nearly half of Arseny’s life, but it passes in just 50 pages in a 360 page book.

The holy fool is a sort of release valve for awkwardness in medieval Russian society: jettison awkwardness by confining it to one particular social type, where it can be contained because it can be expected.

---

In one of the two books of the novel that takes on a traditionally novelistic form, Arseny and his friend Ambrogio travel to Jerusalem, along the way stopping to venerate some relics. In front of the relics of the Venerable Agapit, a renowned healer, Arseny confesses:

> And I am less than my cures, far less, I am not worthy of them, and that makes me feel either frightened or awkward. (III.223)

It’s an interesting confession, because it reveals that Arseny’s awkwardness is not only a tension between him and society, but present in his very being: between his calling and his normal human existence. His healing powers aren’t due to anything he has *cultivated*, but rather simply a part of how God uses him. In this sense, he’s foreign and incomprehensible to himself. The renown and reverence he is given do not reflect the humble, sinful reality that he knows to be true of his life.

---

Toward the very end of the book, there's one final instance of awkwardness:

> Nobody has come to see Laurus since the beginning of August. This does not surprise him. Everyone understands the healing has run out, so they consider visits to Laurus needless. Some might still have come to him but the general mood has spread to them, too. After what they have heard about Laurus in Rukina Quarter, it is somehow awkward to go see him. People feel appearing naïve or—even more disagreeable—like they connive at sin. Laurus is lonely. He did not experience loneliness when he escaped from the world because there had been no feeling of abandonment then. Now the world is escaping from him, which is something entirely different. Laurus is unsettled. (IV.354)

Truly social awkwardness returns here; this time, everyone else feels it with respect to Arseny (now known as Laurus). Not since Christopher’s initial feelings of awkwardness have others feel awkward regarding him. But at the end of his life, he is returning to the beginning: he has visions of Christopher, feels as if Christopher is nearby, wonders if he is truly dead. Laurus’s awkwardness is a sign of his redemption—he has truly taken on the guilt of another, and through it he works toward redeeming Ustina and his son. 

From this perspective, his 21 years of silence as a holy fool, though they were truly a step in the proper direction, were never going to be the sufficient, or the final stage of his life. His failure as a healer returns him to the awkwardness of Ustina’s pregnancy, the awkwardness of his abject failure as a healer to save his own partner and son. Fundamentally, that failure was the result of pride, and his humiliation at the end of his life—the fall of the wise elder Laurus into temptation and sin—is the atonement that he has sought: “For you are awkward, and to awkwardness you shall return.”
