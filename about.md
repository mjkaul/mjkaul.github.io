## A bit more on my self and my beliefs:

&nbsp;

> We must all learn, sooner or later, that we must gather ourselves up & more or less arbitrarily concentrate our interests, throw much overboard to save any.
>
> William James, in a letter to his brother Henry

&nbsp;

Trained in the study of English literature, I have a particular care for language and the ways it intersects with other forms of human community and culture: music, art, and religion; jazz & war; poetry, ritual, & history; politics & architecture. I’m interested in these intersections both in themselves, and inasmuch as they can help us better understand the world we inhabit today. Similarly, I’m particularly interested in language, literature, & history from the nineteenth century to present, though I’m also drawn to exploring the [constellations](https://plato.stanford.edu/entries/benjamin/#BarCon) that our presentation moment makes visible, whether that is with the Stoics, Scripture & the church fathers, certain thinkers of the Renaissance, et cetera.

I have a genuine desire to build a substantial body of knowledge, to read my books well and even to remember them after I’ve read them, and to write lucidly and engagingly about these matters. But it’s also true that forces in my life make the focused attention required by such projects impossible for me without dramatic alteration or radical changes to my lifestyle. Some of these changes are good—like my family and work—others are bad and reflective of some of the most problematic aspects of contemporary American life: a desire to be constantly distracted, a concern for trivialities, and a general laziness that means I’d rather check social media than spend a few minutes making progress toward these more lasting and satisfying projects.

>
> ~ A. G. Sertillanges, O.P.

&nbsp;

So, what to do? First, begin like Montaigne, with total honesty & self-acceptance. (That’s this page.)

Second, cultivating practices of living fully, deeply, & unapologetically the life that I’ve been called to.

Third, read books quickly, thoroughly, deeply, and repeatedly—then *write* about them! (That’s this site.)

Fourth, look for ways to combine work and leisure usefully, staying focused on the work and ideas that I’m best at and that are nearest to my heart. As a writer and editor with a longstanding interest in technology, that means a willingness to learn, and a willingness to take responsibility for the technologies I’m using rather than simply giving my content and so much data on my life to our [technocratic overlords](http://static2.businessinsider.com/image/53e2536c6da811f76320f4f8/young-mark-zuckerberg-allegedly-threw-water-on-an-engineers-computer-and-threatened-employees-with-a-samurai-sword.jpg).

Fifth and finally, ruthlessly, ruthlessly eliminate *everything* else, especially inputs related to news. Cultivate a much deeper & richer “news” cycle based on the long-term goals I hvae. Pursue voluntary hardship wherever possible—especially with respect to information access & consumption.

> Give to any man all the time that he now wastes, not only on his vices (when he has them) but on useless business, wearisome or deteriorating amusements, random reading, & he will have plenty of time for culture. Some of us waste all of it, most of us waste much, but all of us waste some.”
> 
> ~ Matthew Arnold, [*Literature & Dogma*](https://en.wikisource.org/wiki/Literature_and_Dogma)

&nbsp;

> Our ease of access is in itself a blessing, but its misuse can make it a curse. We are all of us tempted to read more poetry & fiction, look at more pictures, listen to more music than we can possibly respond to properly, & the consequence of such overindulgence is not a cultured mind but a consuming one; what it reads, looks at, listens to is immediately forgotten, leaving no more trace than yesterday’s newspaper.
>
> ~ W.H. Auden, [“Culture & Leisure”](http://www.panarchy.org/auden/culture.1966.html)

&nbsp;

To better organize my reading and thinking, and to deepen my writing, I also need to continue reading widely in my areas of interest, but to “read in area clusters,” as [Tyler Cowen advises](http://marginalrevolution.com/marginalrevolution/2017/02/reading-julius-evola.html); that is, read several books or essays, all sharing a theme, together, and write to compare them. This sort of reading not only broadens one’s perspective on that theme; it also demands an eye attentive to details of framing, style, and assumption in order to critically engage the differences between works, and the differences between the works and the truth of the theme. But the benefits, especially deeper learning, are substantial and well worth the cost.

At the same time, I need to write *every day*, responding to my reading as richly and deeply as I’m capable of, and building on the models of those critics and other readers whose own writing I find most [implicative](http://www.academia.edu/12079130/Implicative_Criticism_or_The_Display_of_Thinking). Historian [Thomas Kidd](http://twitter.us3.list-manage.com/subscribe?u=393885e6de8bef994ad9a4c3b&id=f10e42555a) has a daily writing goal of 1,000 words. This is too ambitious given my other commitments, but I do need some similar goal—something to get me going every morning.
